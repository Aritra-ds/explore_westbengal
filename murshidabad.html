History of Murshidabad

Murshidabad, once the capital of Bengal during the Mughal era, holds a regal past marked by Nawabs, palaces, and grand mosques. It was named after Nawab Murshid Quli Khan, who made it the administrative seat in the early 18th century.

The district was a thriving center of silk production, commerce, and politics. Even today, it showcases an impressive mix of Indo-Islamic architecture and cultural richness that reflects the grandeur of Bengal’s historical zenith.

Tourist Attractions

Hazarduari Palace – A 1000-door palace representing royal opulence and Mughal grandeur.

Katra Masjid – A historic mosque built by Nawab Murshid Quli Khan with unique minarets.

Nizamat Imambara – One of the largest Shia congregational halls in India.

Wasif Manzil – A small yet elegant palace with antique charm and Nawabi architecture.

Khoshbagh – The final resting place of Nawab Siraj-ud-Daulah and his family, amidst gardens.

Cultural Heritage

Murshidabad’s music, handicrafts, and Nawabi traditions still echo through its festivals and lifestyle.

Royal Cuisine

Savor rich biryanis and chana bora, inspired by royal kitchens of Bengal.

Silk Legacy

The world-famous Murshidabad silk, woven with delicate craftsmanship, is a treasured legacy.
